Badaling Great Wall Reinforcement Project

Geological Research

Activity Overview

The Badaling Great Wall, located in Yanqing District, Beijing, is both a nationally protected cultural heritage site and a UNESCO World Heritage site. The unstable rock mass beneath the North Seventh Tower poses a collapse risk that threatens the visitor passage below and the structural safety of the historic tower above. As a member of the technical practice team, I applied principles of physics, mathematics, and mechanics to conduct force analysis and stability calculations. Specifically, I participated in estimating the sliding force acting on the unstable rock body and calculating the anti-sliding resistance required for reinforcement. These computations provided a scientific and technical basis for the stabilization of the rock mass, contributing to the safety and preservation of the Great Wall’s heritage structure.

Geophysical Survey of the Longshan Grottoes and Tongzisi Site

Geological Research

Activity Overview

The Longshan Grottoes and Tongzisi Site, located on Longshan Mountain in Jinyuan District, Taiyuan City, Shanxi Province, are both nationally protected cultural heritage sites. The Longshan Grottoes are one of China’s most prominent Taoist grottoes, while the Tongzisi Site preserves five Northern Qi caves and two Tang Dynasty caves. Both sites face serious issues of water seepage and unstable rock mass collapse. As a participant in the field research team, I took part in on-site investigations and data processing, applying geophysical methods such as ground-penetrating radar (GPR), high-density electrical resistivity, and natural electric field detection to determine the distribution of rock fissures and groundwater seepage channels. I also contributed to calculations based on physics, mathematics, and mechanics to estimate the sliding force of unstable rock bodies and the anti-sliding resistance required for reinforcement. The results provided a technical foundation for the engineering design of conservation and stabilization measures, supporting the long-term preservation of these significant cultural heritage sites.

Geophysical Investigation at the Yungang Grottoes

Geological Study

Program Overview

The Yungang Grottoes, located in Datong City, Shanxi Province, are a nationally protected cultural heritage site and a UNESCO World Heritage site. The grottoes face severe crack-induced water seepage problems, posing risks to their preservation. To address these issues, the project employed various geophysical exploration methods, including ground-penetrating radar (GPR), high-density electrical resistivity, borehole seismic surveys, borehole electrical detection, and natural electric field detection. These techniques enabled the identification of water seepage channels within the grotto formations, providing a technical foundation for the design and implementation of waterproofing and protective engineering measures.

Geophysical Survey of the Jindeng Temple Grottoes

Geological Study

Program Overview

Located in Pingshun County, Changzhi City, Shanxi Province, the Jindeng Temple Grottoes are a nationally protected cultural heritage site. The grotto sculptures face deterioration risks caused by karst fissures and groundwater infiltration. This project employed multiple geophysical surveying methods—including ground-penetrating radar (GPR), nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), natural electric field detection, high-density resistivity, and seismic imaging—to investigate the distribution characteristics of karst fissures and cavities within the surrounding rock. The study aimed to identify the pathways of groundwater seepage and provide a scientific basis for the preservation and reinforcement of the grotto structures.

Geophysical Study of the Potala Palace

Geological Research

Activity Overview

Located in Lhasa, Tibet Autonomous Region, the Potala Palace is both a nationally protected cultural heritage site and a UNESCO World Heritage landmark. In recent years, a significant rise in the groundwater level in Lhasa has raised concerns about potential risks to the structural safety and long-term stability of this historic monument. As a member of the field research team, I participated in on-site geophysical investigations and data analysis. Our work involved deploying multiple geophysical exploration techniques, including ground-penetrating radar (GPR), high-density electrical resistivity imaging, and natural electric field detection, to examine the depth and flow direction of groundwater beneath and around the Potala Palace complex. I assisted in data collection, signal interpretation, and visualization of subsurface structures, which were crucial for identifying groundwater pathways. The research provided technical data and a scientific foundation for assessing the effects of groundwater level fluctuations on the preservation and structural integrity of the Palace’s ancient architecture, contributing to the long-term protection of this cultural heritage site.
